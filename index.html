The choice of loss function doesn't just change the math — it determines whether the generator can learn at all during the most critical phase of training. Here's why.

1The discriminator always wins first

In the early stages of GAN training, the discriminator has a structural advantage. It sees both real data and generated noise, and quickly learns to tell them apart. The generator, starting from random weights, produces obvious garbage. During this phase, D(G(z)) ≈ 0 for almost every generated sample — the discriminator is effectively saying "I'm 99% sure this is fake" every single time.

This is the zone on the left side of the chart. It's where training either accelerates or dies, depending entirely on which loss you use.

2What each loss does with that information

Minimax — the original

When D(G(z)) ≈ 0.05, the minimax loss is log(1 − 0.05) = −0.05 — nearly zero, nearly flat. The gradient is −1/(1 − 0.05) ≈ −1.05. That sounds like something, but relative to the gradient later in training (where x → 1 and the gradient → −∞), it's negligible. The generator takes a tiny, nearly useless step.

Non-saturating — the fix

At D(G(z)) ≈ 0.05, the non-saturating loss is −log(0.05) ≈ 3.0 — large and steep. The gradient is −1/0.05 = −20. The generator receives a massive signal: "you are very wrong, here's exactly how wrong." It takes a large, meaningful step toward fooling the discriminator.

3The gradient bar tells the story directly

Switch back to the Loss Curves tab and drag the slider all the way to the left. Watch both gradient bars simultaneously. Non-saturating fills to nearly 100%. Minimax barely registers. Both are looking at the same situation — a discriminator that's confident the sample is fake — but they produce completely opposite gradient magnitudes.

Now drag right toward x = 0.5. The bars converge. By x = 0.8, minimax actually has the larger gradient. But that region is irrelevant in early training — the generator never reaches it until it's already learned to fool the discriminator.

4The practical result

Non-saturating loss trains faster because it eliminates wasted steps

With minimax loss, the first N gradient steps of training are nearly useless — the generator barely updates while the discriminator is dominant. Training appears to do nothing, then suddenly the generator catches up. In practice this leads to instability, mode collapse, and long wall-clock times.

The non-saturating reformulation ensures that every step counts, even at the start. The generator makes meaningful progress on step 1. This stabilizes the adversarial dynamic, reduces the discriminator's lead, and compresses the opening phase of training significantly. It's the same model, same architecture, same learning rate — just a sign flip in the loss.
