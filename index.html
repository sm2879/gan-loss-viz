GAN Generator Loss — Gradient Behavior

The generator learns by receiving gradient feedback. But the gradient only exists if the loss curve has a slope.
Drag the slider to the left to see what happens at the worst moment — when the discriminator is most confident the sample is fake.

Minimax Loss L = log(1 − D(G(z))) original GAN

The curve is nearly flat when D(G(z)) is near 0 — the discriminator easily spots the fake. A flat curve means a near-zero gradient, so the generator gets almost no learning signal exactly when it needs one most.

Non-Saturating Loss L = −log(D(G(z))) Goodfellow et al. 2014 ↗

A simple sign flip. The curve is now steepest where D(G(z)) is near 0 — the generator gets its strongest gradient signal exactly when the discriminator is most confident. Training no longer stalls.

Gradient Magnitude Comparison |∂L/∂x| for both losses on the same axis the key picture

Both losses produce equal gradient at D(G(z)) = 0.5 (crossover). In the shaded zone — where the discriminator is most confident the sample is fake — non-saturating has far more gradient, giving the generator a real signal to improve. Move the dot left to see the divergence grow.
